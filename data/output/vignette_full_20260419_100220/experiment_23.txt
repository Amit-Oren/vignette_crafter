a coherent story but as a relentless, nonverbal replay of horrific visual images—a silent film he
cannot stop or put into words.

This fragmented and intrusive memory process reinforces a core set of negative beliefs about himself

and physiological arousal that accompany these flashbacks—prove he is a “lousy coper.” He ruminates
on how somebody else would not have gotten into this situation, and he has come to a grim conclusion
about human nature: you can never know who will harm you. These appraisals are not abstract; they
are repeatedly confirmed by the visceral reality of his traumatic memories, which feel like present-
tense evidence of his inadequacy and the world’s treachery.

great effort to suppress any thoughts related to the trauma. More broadly, he has ceased making any
plans for the future, as the very act of looking forward feels perilously uncertain. His avoidance
of planning and his hypervigilance in crowds are direct, automatic responses to the triggers that
ambush him and to the threatening sense of the world his appraisals create. The threat he feels is
constant, manifesting in a pervasive difficulty concentrating and a state of physical readiness for

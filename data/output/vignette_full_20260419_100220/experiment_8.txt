**Clinical Vignette**

Mr. R., a 35-year-old divorced man from South America, presents with significant distress following
his military service. His life is now punctuated by moments of intense threat. A cold draft, a
particular song on the radio, or an offhand mention of an ex-partner can instantly trigger a state
of high alert. He becomes hypervigilant, his body reacting with a powerful startle response, as if
the original danger were present. These triggers do more than just provoke fear; they also reinforce
his most painful beliefs about himself. In the wake of such reactions, he is left feeling like a
"lousy coper," isolated from a world that seems to move on without him, and plagued by self-doubt
about his own judgment.

The traumatic events themselves remain a source of profound disturbance. His memories are not just
thoughts but vivid, sensory-laden relivings that feel as if they are happening in the present
moment. He finds these experiences impossible to articulate, trapped in a cycle of nonverbal
recollection. This fragmented and overwhelming memory directly fuels his negative self-appraisals,
convincing him that his reactions are proof of a fundamental personal failing.

To manage the pervasive sense of threat and the painful beliefs it generates, Mr. R. has adopted
several maladaptive strategies. He drinks alcohol to numb his feelings and deliberately avoids
television programmes or films that depict military conflict. When intrusive memories surface, he
makes a concerted effort to push them out of his mind. His PCL-5 score of 43 indicates a clinically
significant level of post-traumatic stress.

Ultimately, his world has become smaller and more perilous. The constant cycling between external
triggers, internalized threat, harsh self-critique, and ineffective coping strategies maintains his
distress. He is caught in a pattern where efforts to control his fear and shame only serve to
reinforce his sense of isolation and inadequacy, leaving him disconnected from the support and
stability he needs.

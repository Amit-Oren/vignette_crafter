**Clinical Vignette: Mr. A**

Mr. A is a 46-year-old, divorced South American man presenting with significant distress following a
violent assault in his hometown two years ago. His daily life is now dominated by a state of high
alert. The sound of a distant siren instantly catapults him into a state of hypervigilance, his body
tensing as if under immediate threat. An unexpected touch from a colleague can trigger a flash of
irritability and a sense of being under siege, making him view ordinary, benign interactions as
suspicious.

These sensory triggers do more than just spike his anxiety; they vividly and involuntarily return
him to the trauma. A siren’s wail can bring back the event with photographic clarity—the visual
details, the sounds, and even the distinct, metallic smell of the alleyway where he was attacked.
These intrusive, olfactory-laden memories are not passive recollections; they feel like a present-
tense reality. In their wake, he is often left with a profound sense of intrinsic fault, a
conviction that there is something fundamentally wrong with him as a person that led to the event
and that ensures nothing good lies ahead for him.

This pervasive belief in his own defectiveness fuels a profound sense of ongoing threat. He
struggles with severe insomnia, his mind too agitated to settle, and he moves through the world in a
state of constant, exhausting watchfulness. To manage this relentless sense of danger and the
painful memories that feed it, Mr. A has adopted a life of stringent avoidance. He no longer goes
anywhere near the neighborhood of the assault. He has withdrawn from social connections, avoiding
any conversation that might touch on the event or his feelings about it. His strategy extends even
to avoiding activities that might elicit strong emotions, whether positive or negative, in a bid to
maintain a numb equilibrium.

Consequently, Mr. A’s world has shrunk dramatically. His PCL-5 score of 42 reflects the significant
functional impairment caused by this cycle. The interplay between his triggered memories, the
negative appraisals they reinforce, and the hypervigilance they sustain, all drive his avoidant
coping. This, in turn, prevents any new, corrective experiences, locking him in a state of perceived
permanent threat and isolation.

Maya, a 50-year-old woman from New Zealand, presents with a severe and debilitating post-trauma
reaction. Her life was irrevocably altered by a traumatic incident involving police, the details of
which she struggles to articulate. She describes her memories not as a coherent story, but as a
series of frozen, sensory-laden snapshots that replay relentlessly in her mind like a broken film.
This fragmented, nonverbal memory of the event is a constant, intrusive presence.

Her world is now saturated with threat. The sight of a uniform or the smell of alcohol on someone’s
breath can trigger an immediate and overwhelming sense of danger, catapulting her back into the
feeling that the trauma is happening again. She lives in a state of hypervigilance, perpetually
superalert and on guard. These triggers do not just provoke fear; they also reinforce a set of
rigid, negative beliefs about herself and the world. She is convinced that people, particularly
those in authority, are fundamentally untrustworthy and not what they seem. She views her own
intense emotional reactions as proof that she is a "lousy coper" and feels a profound sense of
helplessness, believing she cannot stop bad things from happening to her.

These appraisals, in turn, shape her daily existence. The belief in a dangerous and unpredictable
world directly fuels her hypervigilance and the intense negative emotions she endures. Her pervasive
distrust and sense of helplessness mean that ordinary situations, like seeing someone misbehave, are
now interpreted as moral violations and potential threats. To manage this constant sense of peril,
she has adopted extreme safety behaviours. She carries a weapon for protection and goes to great
lengths to avoid any contact with police. She is also consumed by counterfactual thinking, endlessly
ruminating on how her life would be different if the event had never occurred.

The toll is immense. Her marriage ended under the strain, and she is socially isolated. The cycle is
self-perpetuating: her avoidance and hypervigilance prevent any new, corrective experiences, while
the intrusive memories and powerful appraisals keep her psychologically trapped in the past. Her
strategies, though understandable, serve only to maintain her fear and confirm her deepest beliefs
about her own inadequacy and the world’s inherent danger.

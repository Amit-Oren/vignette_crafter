Mrs. A., a 73-year-old European woman, presents with significant distress following a serious
traffic accident two years ago. She describes a world that now feels perpetually unsafe, a state
maintained by a cycle of fear and vigilance. Her daily life is punctuated by specific triggers: the
sound of a car engine, the sight of a bicycle, or the prospect of an interaction with a stranger.
These moments reliably activate a profound sense of threat. She becomes hypervigilant, her
concentration shatters, and she is easily startled by ordinary noises, feeling constantly on guard.

These experiences have solidified into powerful, negative beliefs about herself and the world. When
a trigger occurs, it reinforces her conviction that “somebody else would have stopped the event from
happening,” leading to deep-seated self-blame. She feels she has lost her capacity for normal
emotion, believing “I will never be able to feel normal emotions again.” Most pervasively, she holds
the appraisal that constant vigilance is necessary: “I have to be especially careful because you
never know what can happen next.” This belief directly fuels her ongoing hypervigilance and startle
response, keeping her nervous system in a persistent state of alarm.

In response to these appraisals, Mrs. A. has adopted a range of constricting avoidance strategies.
She no longer travels anywhere near the location of the accident. She refuses to discuss the event
or its aftermath with anyone, including her concerned husband. Furthermore, she has begun to avoid
anything that could stir strong feelings, whether negative or positive, in a bid to prevent
emotional overwhelm. Her world has shrunk considerably as a result.

Her marriage has become strained by her silence and withdrawal. While her husband tries to offer
support, her refusal to talk about the trauma creates distance. The cognitive model illustrates how
her triggers not only generate immediate threat but also activate the negative appraisals that, in
turn, drive both her maladaptive avoidance and sustain her sense of pervasive danger. This self-
perpetuating cycle, rather than the trauma itself, is what currently maintains her PTSD symptoms and
functional impairment.

sudden bereavement of their spouse two years ago. Their life is now dominated by a pervasive sense
of threat. They struggle with insomnia, startling flashbacks that make them feel the loss is

specific triggers: conversations about others losing loved ones, any situation involving
uncertainty, or seeing people who remind them of their spouse.

When these triggers occur, they do more than just provoke anxiety. They directly flood Kai with
intense, sensory-laden memories. Recalling the traumatic event is not a distant recollection; it
feels as if it is happening in the present moment. These memories often include vivid olfactory
sensations from that time and replay in their mind like an unstoppable, intrusive film. The
reactivation of this memory material intensifies their overall sense of threat and directly fuels
their coping behaviors.

Central to Kai’s difficulties are deeply entrenched negative appraisals of themselves and their
world. They feel a crushing hopelessness, convinced they have no future. They view themselves as
fundamentally inadequate and experience a painful isolation, feeling permanently set apart from
others. These beliefs are powerfully activated by the same daily triggers. Furthermore, the
persistent, distressing nature of their traumatic memories continually reinforces these negative
views of themselves.

In response, Kai has adopted several maladaptive strategies. They actively try to suppress any
trauma-related thoughts and go to great lengths to avoid people or situations that might remind them
of the event. They also spend excessive time dwelling on how life used to be before the loss. This
avoidance and rumination are direct attempts to manage the overwhelming sense of threat generated by
their symptoms and are shaped by their negative self-concept. Unfortunately, these strategies
prevent emotional processing, locking Kai in a cycle where triggers lead to distressing memories and
appraisals, which in turn fuel more avoidance and heightened threat, maintaining their severe PTSD
symptoms.

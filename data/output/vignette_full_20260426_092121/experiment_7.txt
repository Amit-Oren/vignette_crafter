**Clinical Vignette: Rosa**

Rosa is a 71-year-old Latin American woman who works as a carpenter and presents with a PCL-5 score
of 34 following a life-threatening illness. During her medical crisis, she endured prolonged
hospitalisation, invasive procedures, and periods of complete physical helplessness — experiences
that left her body feeling alien and her trust in others profoundly shaken. The peak of her terror
came during a procedure in which she was rendered temporarily immobile, surrounded by unfamiliar
clinical staff, in a room of absolute silence broken only by machinery.

In session, Rosa does not describe her illness abstractly. She speaks in fragments, returning
repeatedly to a single frozen image: her own hands — the hands of a skilled carpenter — lying
useless at her sides on the hospital table. She struggles to place this image in sequence with what
came before or after; it simply arrives, unannounced, cutting into her present.

Her central stuck point is organised around three convictions she articulates quietly but with
certainty: that her body has become something shameful, something that failed her and now marks her;
that other people, including medical professionals and those closest to her, cannot ultimately be
relied upon; and that her inability to simply *get over it* proves she is a poor coper by nature. "A
strong woman would have moved past this by now," she says.

These beliefs keep her system in a state of readiness. Her conviction that people cannot be trusted
means she is immediately on guard when someone moves suddenly near her workbench — a colleague
reaching across for a tool is enough to spike her heart rate and flood her with physical reactivity.
Driven by her negative appraisals of her own fragility and others' untrustworthiness, she scans her
environment continuously, and this threat monitoring in turn reinforces her sense that the world
remains dangerous, feeding back into her core beliefs. Sudden silence — the kind that falls when a
power tool is switched off — stops her mid-task, pulling her back to that hospital room.

Paradoxically, the strategies Rosa uses to manage this distress compound her difficulties. She
smokes steadily through her working day, and she has begun taking physical risks in her carpentry
work — working without adequate guards, lifting loads beyond safe limits — in ways she cannot fully
explain. She defers constantly to colleagues and her partner, going along with whatever is suggested
rather than asserting her own preferences. These patterns, driven by her appraisals of herself as
inadequate and others as untrustworthy, lead to further evidence that she cannot manage: the risk-
taking frightens her, the compliance leaves her feeling invisible, and the result is a deepened
conviction that she is, as she puts it, "a lousy coper." This self-reinforcing loop — in which
threat appraisal drives maladaptive coping, and that coping in turn produces outcomes that confirm
her negative self-appraisals, which then sustain her threat sensitivity — forms the dominant
maintaining cycle in Rosa's presentation. She worries obsessively about harm befalling her partner,
checking in repeatedly by phone from the workshop, unable to tolerate uncertainty about those she
loves.

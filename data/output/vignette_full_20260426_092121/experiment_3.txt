# Clinical Case Vignette: Rajan

## Presenting Complaints and Trauma Account

Rajan is a 49-year-old South Asian man currently employed as a call centre agent, presenting with a
PCL-5 score of 74 following a street robbery approximately eight months ago. He was approached from
behind by two men on a motorcycle while walking home from a late shift. One assailant grabbed him by
the collar, pressed something hard against his ribs, and demanded his wallet and phone. Rajan
describes freezing completely — unable to call out, unable to run — while the motorcycle engine
idled beside him. The men fled within seconds. The brevity of the encounter has done nothing to
diminish its grip on him. Because the attack was announced by the sound of a motorcycle pulling
close and executed by strangers who appeared without warning in a public street, the specific
sensory and spatial features of that moment remain acutely loaded.

## Memory Phenomenology

Rajan does not recount the robbery so much as re-enter it. When asked to describe what happened, he
falls quiet and shakes his head slowly: *"I know what happened — I was there — but when I try to say
it, the words just don't come."* He can gesture to his ribs, can mime the freeze, but the experience
resists narrative. What returns instead is the terror itself: during one session, the distant sound
of a motorbike in the car park outside caused him to grip the chair arms, his breathing visibly
shallow, the fear arriving at full intensity rather than as recollection.

## Central Stuck Point

The robbery confirmed something Rajan had perhaps feared but never fully believed: that he is
fundamentally unable to protect himself or those he loves. In his own words: *"A normal man would
have done something. I just stood there. There is something wrong with me."* He describes himself as
permanently changed — *"I used to laugh, I used to enjoy things. That person is gone."* He holds the
conviction that bad things will continue to find him, that his helplessness that night was not
situational but revealing.

## Threat Monitoring and Avoidance

At work, Rajan sits with his back to the wall and faces the office entrance throughout his shift. He
no longer takes calls that involve raised or urgent voices without first scanning the room. He has
stopped using the route home that passes the site of the robbery and avoids the surrounding
neighbourhood entirely. Positive moments — a joke shared with a colleague, a comfortable evening at
home — are cut short internally; he pushes the feeling away before it can settle.

## Maintaining Cycle

The dominant maintaining cycle operates through the mutual reinforcement of triggers, memory, and
appraisal. When the sound of a motorcycle reaches Rajan — whether on his commute or audible through
the call centre windows — the terror returns at full force rather than as a distant memory, which in
turn reactivates his belief that he is defective and permanently helpless. That appraisal then
amplifies his sensitivity to the very triggers that provoked it: streets, strangers, engine sounds
all become more threatening once he has reminded himself that he cannot cope. His avoidance of going
out and his relentless environmental scanning are the behavioural expression of these beliefs —
present in his daily life but not their cause. The cycle tightens around him quietly, leaving his
partner increasingly concerned by his withdrawal, and his performance at work compromised by the
vigilance that now accompanies every shift.

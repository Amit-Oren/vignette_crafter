## Threat Monitoring and Avoidance

As a Policy Analyst, Priya regularly works late in a government building where corridors fall silent
after six o'clock. That silence reliably leads her to turn on a small radio at her desk, the sound
filling the space before the dread can settle. At home, she sleeps with lights on. She has declined
two routine health appointments since the bereavement and avoids any route that passes the hospital
where her son was taken. She has removed all photographs of her son from her office and her home.
She keeps alcohol in her desk drawer.

## Maintaining Factors

The dominant maintaining cycle runs through negative appraisals, threat, and memory. Priya's
conviction that she is weak and failed her son keeps her nervous system in a state of unresolvable
vigilance, in which nothing around her feels genuinely safe and no external reassurance registers.
That sustained state of unresolvable vigilance means that her body and mind remain primed for
catastrophe, resulting in repeated intrusions of somatic pain and imageless emotional flooding — the
fragmented memory traces of that night breaking through without warning. Her intrusive experiences
and her appraisals of inadequacy are both present in her daily life; she holds them as parallel
certainties. Her avoidance of grief reminders and her alcohol use interrupt any natural processing,
ensuring the memory remains fragmented and unintegrated. The cycle is self-sealing: the very
strategies Priya employs to manage her distress at work and at home prevent the disconfirmation her
appraisals urgently require.

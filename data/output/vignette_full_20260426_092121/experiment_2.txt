**Clinical Case Vignette: "Darius"**

Darius is a 32-year-old single South-East Asian male employed as a social ministry worker. He was
referred for assessment following a workplace incident approximately fourteen months ago in which he
accidentally caused serious physical injury to a vulnerable client during what was intended as a
supportive intervention. At the moment of peak danger — the instant he registered that the person
had been hurt and that his own actions were the immediate cause — Darius reports freezing entirely,
unable to call for help or move toward the injured individual. His PCL-5 score of 80 indicates
severe and pervasive post-traumatic symptomatology across all clusters.

Memory of the event does not come to Darius as a coherent narrative. When asked to describe what
happened, he trails off mid-sentence, losing the thread of the account and falling silent, sometimes
mid-word. When the memory does surface — unbidden, in the middle of a client meeting — he is
overwhelmed so rapidly that he has to excuse himself, unable to remain present. He describes the
intrusion as a sudden forced return, as though the event insists on re-entering without permission.

The stuck point at the centre of Darius's presentation is one of moral rupture. In his own words:
*"I did something that someone like me would never do. I don't know who I am now."* He holds the
event as proof that he caused harm through his own conduct — a violation of the values that had
previously organised his sense of self and his choice of vocation. When specific reminders of the
incident surface, such as a colleague's unexpected touch or a client reaching out for a hug, they
caused a sharp resurgence of self-blame and the conviction that his actions revealed something
irreparably wrong in him. These same sensory cues led to immediate avoidance: Darius restructures
sessions, declines home visits, and works hard to suppress any thought that returns him to the
event.

Darius cannot settle even in his own home; his body remains braced and scanning. His appraisals of
self-blame and violated identity keep his threat system active, leaving him in a state of chronic
physiological arousal. Sensory reminders of the incident — a hand placed briefly on his shoulder
during a team debrief, a client leaning in — trigger immediate alarm responses in his body,
resulting in his nervous system treating ordinary workplace contact as a signal of danger. That
sustained physiological arousal caused intrusive recollections of the event to break through at
unpredictable moments, pulling him out of conversations and client sessions without warning. His
efforts to suppress these memories and avoid emotionally demanding situations — strategies he relies
on heavily — mean that the memory of the event is never processed, leaving it prone to forced re-
entry.

The dominant maintaining cycle is clear: chronic threat monitoring keeps Darius physiologically
primed, which means that routine physical and interpersonal cues continue to register as dangerous.
Those alarm responses produce further avoidance and thought suppression, which in turn sustain the
intrusive memory rather than diminishing it. Darius dwells at length on who he was before the
incident. He has not taken on a new case in six weeks, and his colleagues have noticed his
withdrawal from the shared, hands-on culture of care that defines their team.

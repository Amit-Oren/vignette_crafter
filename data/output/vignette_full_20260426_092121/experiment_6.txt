# Clinical Case Vignette

**Patient: Aroha, 57-year-old Māori woman, Train Conductor, Divorced**

---

Aroha presented following a toxic chemical exposure incident that occurred approximately fourteen
months prior, when a freight container aboard a train she was conducting was found to be leaking an
unidentified industrial solvent. Before emergency services could evacuate the area, Aroha spent an
estimated twenty-three minutes in the contaminated cab, unable to open the jammed exterior door —
the moment of peak danger being when she felt her airways constrict and lost partial consciousness
against the cab window. She was hospitalised for six days. Her PCL-5 score of 49 indicates
clinically significant PTSD symptomatology.

Aroha describes being pulled back without warning to the sensation of her chest seizing — not a
narrative recollection, but a sudden bodily re-experience. She does not report it as a memory so
much as something that *happens to her again*: her throat tightens, her hands grip whatever surface
is nearest, and several seconds pass before she can locate herself in the present. During clinical
interview she paused mid-sentence when the ventilation system shifted audibly, pressing her palm
flat against the table as if steadying herself.

Her central stuck point is held in three interlocking convictions she articulates with quiet
certainty: *"My lungs will never be right again. I couldn't get out, and I'll never be able to stop
something like that happening again. I don't know what I thought the world was before — it wasn't
this."* The exposure did not merely injure her body; it dismantled the framework through which she
had understood safety and agency.

Aroha's hypervigilance is expressed in highly specific, practical terms. Before sitting in any
enclosed space — a staff room, a waiting area, a GP's consulting room — she locates the exits. She
wakes at night and moves through her flat checking windows and doors before she can return to sleep.
On the platform, when a passing freight locomotive emits any burnt or acrid smell, her body responds
before she has consciously registered the odour: heart rate accelerates, breathing shortens, and she
is already stepping backward. The smell of antiseptic in the occupational health clinic produces the
same involuntary bracing.

The dominant maintaining cycle in Aroha's presentation centres on the connection between her
persistent threat monitoring and her negative appraisals. The constant scanning of her environment
for chemical odours, blocked exits, and respiratory threat continuously confirms to her that danger
is both imminent and uncontrollable. Each night-check, each mapped escape route in a new train cab,
each moment her body fires before her mind catches up — all of these feed directly back into her
conviction that she is permanently damaged and fundamentally unable to protect herself. The
hypervigilance that was adaptive in the cab has become the very mechanism that keeps her beliefs
about her own fragility and the world's hostility freshly reinforced, shift after shift.

post-traumatic stress symptoms after a road incident in which he accidentally caused serious injury
to another person. He describes the peak moment as the sudden impact, the person on the ground, and
his own failure to react quickly enough to help in the way he believes he should have. In his mind,
that single scene has become the point at which he decided he was “the sort of person this kind of
thing happens through.”

When Aung speaks about the event, he does not tell it in a smooth sequence. He stops, circles back,
and describes only fragments: the sound, the body falling, a flash of headlights, and then nothing
clear in between. At times he says it feels as if the frightening scene is happening right now, not
years ago. He struggles to place the pieces in order, and the gaps themselves seem to unsettle him
more than the details he can recall.

Aung’s central stuck point is that the accident proved he is fundamentally at fault, and that the
person he has become is no longer recognisable to him. He speaks in a close paraphrase of, “This
happened because of the kind of man I am. I can’t handle even the smallest upset, so I am not who I
thought I was.”

In daily life, Aung is hyper-alert to danger. Even when colleagues reassure him that he is safe, he
says he cannot believe it. At work, in meetings at the planning office, he prefers to sit with his
back against a wall so he can watch the whole room. He has difficulty concentrating on reports and
drawings, his attention repeatedly pulled toward possible harm to people he cares about. He has also
been avoiding starting new friendships or relationships, and since his divorce he has kept social
contact limited and controlled.

The dominant pattern is self-reinforcing. His harsh appraisals feed his sense that the world is
unsafe and that he cannot cope, which makes the accident feel more immediate and more fragmented in
memory. That disorganised recollection then strengthens his belief that something in him is
defective or blameworthy. His fear response keeps him scanning rooms, doubting reassurance, and
holding himself at a distance from others, and his detachment from the memory leaves him with even
less access to a settled account of what happened. In practice, this affects his work relationships:
in open-plan project meetings he sits rigidly at the wall, misses parts of discussion, and withdraws
from informal lunches, reinforcing his isolation.

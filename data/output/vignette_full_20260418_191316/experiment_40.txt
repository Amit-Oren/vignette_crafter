stress symptoms following the bereavement of a close loved one. His PCL-5 score of 47 is consistent
with clinically significant distress. He describes a pattern of living on edge, with physical touch,

reactions, and brief episodes in which he suddenly feels and acts as though the event is happening
again.

His recollections of the bereavement are fragmentary and image-based. He says the memories arrive as
pictures rather than a coherent story, and that he cannot easily put the experience into words. When
he remembers the event, it feels immediate and present rather than in the past. These memory
intrusions are a central part of his daily suffering, repeatedly pulling him back into the emotional
and bodily state associated with the loss.

He has developed harsh beliefs about what his reactions mean. He worries that the intensity of his
symptoms means he is going crazy, feels that nothing good can happen to him anymore, and doubts that
he can trust himself to do the right thing. These appraisals sit at the centre of his presentation,
shaping how he interprets his experience and how he tries to manage it. He spends much of the day
trying not to think about the trauma, keeping his mind occupied as much as possible, and avoiding
reminders when others bring up the event.

Despite these efforts, the symptoms remain self-perpetuating. When he is physically activated, the
intrusive memories become more vivid and immediate, and the memories in turn intensify his threat

his memories take, leaving him caught in a cycle of fear, vigilance, and mental strain. The overall
picture is one of narrowing daily life, strained confidence, and emotional isolation, with reminders
of the loss continuing to dominate his sense of self and safety.
